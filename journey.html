Creator Journey

From beginner to builder.

The Syndicate Exoplanet Archive is not only a 6,100 planet NFT collection. It is the record of a creator learning how to turn public science, AI-assisted study, Python, Blender, metadata, GitHub, and long-term worldbuilding into one focused launch.

Why this page matters

The work behind the archive.

The collection began as a broad idea and became a working archive through months of research, testing, mistakes, corrections, and rebuilds. The goal was never to let AI do the project alone. The goal was to use AI as a learning partner while still making the creative decisions, choosing the direction, building the visual identity, and pushing the collection toward a real launch.

This page is here so collectors can see the human work behind the digital art. Every planet card, metadata field, rarity decision, website correction, and Blender render is part of that journey.

Research

Learning from public science

I spent a little over six months researching different aspects of exoplanets before this collection started to feel real. NASA’s websites became a fundamental part of that process because they helped me understand how planets are discovered, how scientists classify them, and why public exoplanet data matters. I spent hundreds of hours online reading, comparing, and organizing information so the archive would not feel random or disconnected from real astronomy. The goal was to build a science-fiction collection with a scientific foundation underneath it. The artwork is fictional and cinematic, but the idea begins with the real search for worlds beyond our Solar System. That research gave the collection its structure: names, discovery methods, planet classes, rarity logic, moons, rings, and archive identity. It also helped me see the bigger picture. These planets are not just images. They are creative interpretations of humanity’s ongoing search for other worlds.

Code

Python, metadata, and site building

Coding became one of the biggest learning curves in the entire project. I used ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini to help me understand programming, especially Python, but I still consider myself a beginner. I am not trying to pretend I am an expert. What matters to me is that I kept learning and kept building. Python helped connect the exoplanet catalog, the metadata, the render rules, and the website structure into something that could actually support a full collection. Building the metadata from the NASA Exoplanet Archive was a huge learning experience by itself. It forced me to think about naming, traits, rarity scores, image paths, token structure, and how collectors would understand each planet. Every script and every correction taught me something new. The code became more than a tool. It became the bridge between raw science data and a finished NFT archive.

Blender

Turning data into planet renders

Before this project, I had never used Blender. Learning it from scratch was frustrating, exciting, and honestly overwhelming at times. I watched so many videos on rendering planets, lighting scenes, building textures, creating shadows, working with rings, and controlling cameras that there were moments where I thought I would never be able to do it. Eventually, I realized that Python could help turn Blender into a repeatable render system for the collection. That changed the direction of the project. Instead of making one planet at a time by hand, I started learning how data, scripts, and visual rules could work together. I want people to see this as a different kind of AI-assisted journey. AI helped me learn, troubleshoot, and organize, but it did not replace the work. The goal was to build skills, not skip them. The final renders are the result of persistence, testing, mistakes, and learning how to make the archive visually consistent.

Launch

GitHub, marketplace prep, and collection strategy

As the collection moves toward launch, I hope people can see the countless hours that went into building it. Over the last six months, I have been learning how to take a broad idea and focus it into a 6,100 planet NFT collection with a website, metadata, preview graphics, rarity structure, and long-term story direction. GitHub became part of that journey because I had to learn how to update files, test changes, fix broken paths, and keep the site moving forward one correction at a time. Marketplace preparation has also pushed me to think beyond the art. A real collection needs clear language, protected previews, working pages, launch timing, collector trust, and a strategy that makes sense. In the end, I want people to appreciate both sides of the project: the digital art itself and the journey it took to build it from nothing into something ready for the public.
